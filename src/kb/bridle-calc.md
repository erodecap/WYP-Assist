# Bridle Calculator

The Bridle Calculator computes leg lengths, angles, and tensions for two-leg bridle rigging configurations. It ensures your bridle angles stay within safe limits.

## Getting Started

1. Enter the **load weight** at the bridle point
2. Enter the **span** between the two attachment points
3. Enter the **drop distance** (vertical distance from attachment to load)
4. Review the calculated angles, tensions, and leg lengths

## Key Concepts

### Bridle Angle
The angle each leg makes from vertical. **Never exceed 60 degrees** — as the angle increases, tension in each leg increases dramatically.

### Tension Factor
At 0° (straight down), each leg carries half the load. As the angle increases:
- **30°** — Each leg carries 58% of total load
- **45°** — Each leg carries 71% of total load
- **60°** — Each leg carries 100% of total load (danger zone)

### Leg Length
The actual length of cable, sling, or chain needed for each bridle leg, calculated from the geometry.

## Reading Results

The calculator displays:
- **Bridle angle** — The angle from vertical for each leg
- **Leg tension** — Force in each bridle leg
- **Leg length** — Required length of each sling/cable
- **Safety warnings** — Alerts when angles exceed safe limits

## Color Indicators

- **Green** — Angle is within safe operating range (under 45°)
- **Yellow** — Angle is approaching limits (45°-55°)
- **Red** — Angle is at or beyond maximum safe angle (over 55°)

## Tips

- Keep bridle angles under 45° whenever possible
- If angles exceed 60°, reposition attachment points or add a third point
- Always use the rated capacity of your shortest/weakest component
- Account for the weight of the bridle hardware itself in your calculations
- Verify attachment point capacities can handle the calculated leg tensions
